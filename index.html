📚Knowledge Box — The Financial SystemRead me first! ▼

The financial system moves money between savers and borrowers through four distinct components.

🏦 Financial Institutions — the ORGANISATIONS

The businesses themselves: commercial banks, building societies, insurance companies, pension funds, hedge funds, credit unions, central banks.

⚠️ Institutions are the organisations — not what they trade. Barclays is an institution. Shares are not.

📈 Financial Markets — the PLACES where trading happens

The "venues": Stock Market, Bond Market, Forex Market, Commodity Markets, Derivatives Market, Money Markets, Futures Market, ETF Exchange, Crypto Market.

💡 The Stock Market is where shares are traded — it is the market, not the share itself.

📄 Financial Instruments — the ASSETS being traded

Equity: Shares · Debt: Bonds, Debentures, Treasury Bills, Certificates of Deposit · Derivatives: Options, Futures Contracts · Funds: ETFs, Cash.

⚠️ Don't confuse market (the place) with instrument (the thing). Stock Market = market. Shares = instrument.

🛎️ Financial Services — activities that SUPPORT transactions

Financial Planning, Wealth Management, Investment Advisory, Insurance Services, Payment Processing, Remittance, Custody Services, Leasing Services, Credit Card Services.

💡 If someone is doing something finance-related for you — advising, processing, insuring — that's a service.

📚Knowledge Box — Sources of FinanceRead me first! ▼

🏢 Internal Finance — money already INSIDE the business

No new obligations. Usually cheapest. Key sources: Retained Earnings, Sale of Assets, Working Capital Management, Depreciation Provisions.

⚠️ Retained earnings are INTERNAL even though they come from sales. The test: was the money already inside the business before this financing decision?

🌐 External Finance — money from OUTSIDE the business

Creates new obligations — repayments to lenders or ownership dilution for investors. Split by duration:

⚡ Short-Term (<1 year) — for cash flow gaps

Bank Overdraft · Trade Credit · Debt Factoring · Invoice Discounting · Revolving Credit · Peer-to-Peer Lending

📅 Medium-Term (1–5 years) — for equipment & growth

Leasing · Hire Purchase · Bank Loan · Venture Capital · Crowdfunding · Asset Finance

🔒 Long-Term / Permanent — for buildings & ownership

Share Issue · Debenture · Corporate Bond · Mortgage · Government Grants · Angel Investment

💡 Short = cash flow gaps · Medium = buying equipment · Long = buildings & permanent capital

📚Knowledge Box — Debt vs Equity & the P&LRead me first! ▼

⚖️ Debt Finance

Borrowed money — must be repaid with interest. Examples: bank loans, bonds, debentures, overdrafts. Gearing = debt ÷ equity. High gearing = more debt = higher financial risk. Debt holders are paid before shareholders in liquidation.

📊 Equity Finance

Raising money by issuing shares — no mandatory repayment. Ordinary shareholders get voting rights and variable dividends. Preference shareholders get fixed dividends but usually no voting rights. A Rights Issue offers new shares to existing shareholders at a discount.

📋 The Critical Rule — Where things sit in the accounts

✅ Interest expense → P&L as a Finance Cost (NOT operating expenses)
❌ Dividends paid → NOT in P&L → reduce Retained Earnings on Balance Sheet
❌ Share capital issued → NOT an expense → increases Equity on Balance Sheet
❌ Loan repayments → NOT in P&L → reduce a Liability on Balance Sheet
⚠️ If the money flows between the company and its funders, it goes on the Balance Sheet — not the P&L.

📋 P&L Structure

Revenue − Cost of Sales = Gross Profit
− Operating Expenses = Operating Profit (EBIT)
− Finance Costs (interest only) = Net Profit Before Tax

🏦 Bond vs Debenture

Bond Usually secured on assets → lower risk → lower interest rate. Debenture Usually unsecured → slightly higher risk for investors.

📚Knowledge Box — IPO & Capital MarketsRead me first! ▼

🆕 Primary Market — NEW securities being issued

Fresh capital flows directly to the issuer. Includes: IPO, Rights Issue, new bond/debenture issues, government gilt issuances.

⚠️ One test: Is a brand-new security being created and does money go to the issuer? → Primary market.

🔄 Secondary Market — EXISTING securities traded between investors

No new capital reaches the company. Any trade on a stock exchange after the IPO listing date is secondary — ownership changes hands, nothing goes to the firm.

💡 The stock exchange is mostly a secondary market. The IPO is primary. All trades after listing are secondary.

📋 Key IPO Terms

  • Underwriter Investment bank managing the IPO — sets price, buys unsold shares
  • Prospectus Legal document with financials & risks — must be published before IPO
  • Book Building Collecting investor bids to determine the final offer price
  • Price Band The min–max range investors can bid within
  • Lock-Up Period Insiders cannot sell shares for ~6 months post-IPO
  • Greenshoe Option Underwriter can sell up to 15% extra shares if demand is high
  • Free Float % of shares available for public trading
  • IPO Discount Offer price set below true value to ensure all shares sell
  • Dilution Existing shareholders' % falls when new shares are issued
  • Roadshow Management tour pitching the IPO to institutional investors
  • Allotment Assigning shares to investors after subscription closes
  • Grey Market Unofficial trading of IPO shares before official listing
  • Market Cap Share Price × Total Shares = total company value
  • Pre-IPO Placement Shares sold to select investors before the public IPO

📚Knowledge Box — IPO CalculationsRead me first! ▼

A company doesn't keep everything raised — IPO costs come off first. Three steps:

Step 1 — Gross Proceeds: Shares × IPO Offer Price
Step 2 — Underwriting Fee: Gross Proceeds × Fee %
Step 3 — Net Proceeds: Gross Proceeds − Underwriting Fee − Other Expenses
⚠️ Net Proceeds is always less than Gross Proceeds. It's the actual cash available for business use.

📌 Worked Example

2,000,000 shares at $10 · Fee 5% · Other expenses $200,000

  • Gross Proceeds = 2,000,000 × $10 = $20,000,000
  • Underwriting Fee = $20,000,000 × 0.05 = $1,000,000
  • Net Proceeds = $20m − $1m − $0.2m = $18,800,000
💡 No commas when typing. Enter 10000000 not 10,000,000 or the answer will be rejected.

🏦 What is the Underwriting Fee?

The investment bank guarantees the IPO raises its full target — buying any unsold shares itself. The fee (3–7%) is their reward for taking that risk.
